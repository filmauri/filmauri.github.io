How to learn everything

Posted on Sep 19, 2021

​ We all have shabby musical instruments, dusty sports equipment, a few barely scratched language texts, the too-long pants that have lived in your closet for half a decade and will teach you how to hem someday…

The desire to learn something new arises from that best and most curious part of us. But then we have to get down to business. When it’s day three on the keyboard and the cat who walks the keys still sounds better than we do, we can get discouraged - and often give up.

Writer Josh Kaufman, author of The First 20 Hours: How to Learn Anything … Fast and The Personal MBA: Master the Art of Business, has figured out why so many of us get stopped in our tracks during this first period of learning. “Feeling stupid is not good, and the beginning of learning anything new is feeling stupid,” she says.

Through trial and error, she found four steps that can help you climb the steep slope of the learning curve in less than 20 hours. Why 20? As he says, “20 hours is doable - that’s about 45 minutes a day for about a month, even skipping a couple of days here and there.”

Break down a skill into its components.

The first thing you need to do is decide what you want to learn, and then break it down into smaller, more manageable pieces. Let’s say you want to bake your own bread. This is a multi-step process that includes preparing the dough, rising, punching, forming a loaf, and baking. You’ll start by identifying the different tools and skills behind each step.

Or, if you choose yoga as your new hobby, start at home with a video that shows you the basic poses and breathing techniques - and then go try a class.

Learn enough to know when you’re making a mistake.

“Get three to five resources on what you’re trying to learn,” Kaufman says. “They could be books, they could be DVDs, they could be anything, but don’t use them as a way to procrastinate.” After all, you won’t learn how to bake bread or do yoga if you don’t get out the flour or yoga mat and do something.

Set a limit on the number of resources you’re consulting - you don’t need to buy every book or watch every YouTube video on the topic; there’s time for that later - and jump in.

Remove all barriers to practice.

This may require putting away your electronic devices while you tackle your hobby. Or get creative and combine your favorite distraction with your new activity. In a TEDxPenn talk, behavioral scientist Katherine Milkman advocates a technique called “temptation bundling”: pairing something you know you love to do with something you’re trying to get yourself to do. Turn on your favorite podcast while you cook, or you could turn your weekly coffee with a friend into a weekly at-home yoga session with the two of you.

Exercise for at least 20 hours.

To overcome what Kaufman calls the “frustration barrier” - that period in the beginning when you’re painfully incompetent and you know it - you need to commit to continuing your new activity for at least 20 hours. At that point, he says, “you’ll be amazed at how good you are.”
